Attention First
Attention is the only thing we actually own.
I want to help people focus on their own attention. In the AI era, attention is the only thing we actually own. Information is popping up from every direction, and our focus loses its way.
So we train on two dimensions
🛡️
Build your inner world deeply.
That's what protects you through the great shift. You don't need to be perfect — just keep shaping your own view.
📡
Keep your ears open.
Things move fast. You need steady input to make sense of it.
"English isn't really my subject — attention is."
This is where language comes in. Practicing English in international settings happens to be one of the best attention workouts you can do. Every conversation forces you to choose what to focus on, what to let go, and what to commit to.
One thing upfront
I'm not an English teacher. If you want one, go find a trained educator. What I do is help you use English in the real world. You don't come to me to learn the language — you come to practice attention through it.
Here's what I've learned
1
Stop chasing textbook English.
Even Korean speakers who sound almost native lose the thread in real meetings — because their attention is on sounding correct, not on the room. 1.5 billion people use English; 1.12 billion are non-native. Aim your attention at them, not at the textbook.
2
Find your own unit.
We can only hold 3–4 chunks at once. Don't fixate on words — that scatters your attention. Stretch your unit instead: word → phrase → sentence → paragraph → story. The size of your unit is the size of your attention.
3
Learn like a baby — but smarter.
Babies don't use books. They learn through emotion and interaction, with full attention on one thing at a time. Adults can't actually become babies again, but we can borrow the principle: pick one word or pattern a day and give it your real attention. Play with it.
4
Sit with the discomfort.
American-style English forces you to take a position before you fully understand the topic. That feels wrong to us — Korean attention likes to circle, gather, then decide. Notice the discomfort. Stay in it. That's the training.
5
Use AI as a mirror.
Write something in Korean. Ask AI to translate it. Then ask it to rewrite it the American way. Feel the difference. Korean attention balances first, then decides. English attention decides first, then supports. Practicing the flip is practicing attentional flexibility.
6
Language isn't about intelligence.
It's about familiarity, and familiarity is just attention repeated over time. Chunk it. Memorize it. Repeat it. That's the whole entry into the language world — and the whole training for the AI one.
7
Use two different spaces.
Keep talking to AI — that's where your language patterns surface and get refined. It's safe, endlessly patient, and free of judgment. Conversations with people are different. That's where you go to understand emotions and ideas. Don't try to fix your English there. Look at the person instead.
